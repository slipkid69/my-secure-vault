Black Magic & Witchcraft – The Serpent’s Power in the Hands of Man
1. The Origin: From Eden to Babylon
Witchcraft and black magic are not new. Their roots go back to the Garden of Eden, when Satan, the serpent, whispered the first great lie:
“Ye shall be as gods, knowing good and evil.” – Genesis 3:5
This was the birth of occultism: seeking forbidden knowledge, power apart from God, and divinity through rebellion.
• Nimrod and Babylon (Genesis 10–11): The post-Flood world consolidated rebellion at Babel, building a tower to heaven and forming the first unified occult empire. Nimrod, Cush, and Semiramis become the archetypes of false gods and goddesses in every major occult system since.
“Babylon is fallen… she became the habitation of devils…” — Revelation 18:2
This is where astrology, enchantments, necromancy, idol worship, and blood rituals began.

2. Ancient World Systems
Witchcraft and sorcery flourished in ancient civilisations:
• Egypt: The magicians of Pharaoh replicated Moses’ signs through dark arts (Exodus 7:11–12). Egypt venerated Thoth, god of magic, and practiced ritual death, curses, and divination.
• Canaan: The Canaanite religions involved child sacrifice to Molech and sexual rites to Asherah. God commanded Israel to completely destroy these nations because of their witchcraft.
• Greece and Rome: Practiced necromancy, consulted oracles, invoked Hecate, the triple goddess of the underworld. Witches and seers were part of statecraft.
“There shall not be found among you… one who uses divination, or is an enchanter… or a witch.”
— Deuteronomy 18:10–12
These practices were not merely misguided—they were abominations.

3. Black Magic Defined
Black Magic is the intentional use of spiritual power to manipulate, control, harm, or destroy.
Key aspects:
• Curses and hexes
• Blood rituals – animal and human sacrifice
• Binding spells – controlling someone’s will
• Demonic invocation – inviting spirits to possess or act on behalf of the practitioner
• Sympathetic magic – voodoo dolls, effigies, sigils
• Divination – tarot, astrology, crystal scrying
Unlike folk magic or “white” magic (a false distinction), black magic is overtly destructive, often directed by demonic forces in exchange for power.

4. Witchcraft Through the Ages
Medieval & Renaissance Europe
• Witch trials (e.g., Salem, Pendle, Würzburg) often reflect societal hysteria, but genuine practitioners existed.
• The rise of the Grimoire tradition: spellbooks like The Lesser Key of Solomon and The Picatrix—manuals of summoning and spirit contact.
• The Church, though compromised at times, rightly condemned sorcery as satanic rebellion.
Modern Resurgence
• 19th century: Theosophy (Blavatsky), Golden Dawn (Crowley), and rise of ceremonial magic
• 20th century: Wicca (Gerald Gardner), Neopaganism, chaos magic
• Pop culture: Normalisation of witchcraft in TV, film, music, and fashion—especially among youth
• Internet occultism: TikTok witches, YouTube spellcasters, Discord covens
