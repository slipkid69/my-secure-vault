⟁ 2.1: Babel and the Religion of the Heavens
The Tower of Babel (Genesis 11) was more than an architectural rebellion. It was the first organised occult religion after the flood.
They said:
“Let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven…”
— Genesis 11:4
This was not just height—it was astral alignment.
Historians and scholars suggest the ziggurats of Mesopotamia were designed to commune with “the gods,” tracking celestial bodies and calling down divine knowledge.
The tower was a spiritual antenna—a rejection of Yahweh’s sovereignty and an attempt to merge heaven and earth through human means.

The Whore of Babylon in Revelation (Rev 17) is not just a city—it is this very system of spiritual adultery, now globalised.

The Origin: Babel and the First Global Rebellion
After the flood, mankind united under Nimrod, the great-grandson of Noah. He was a mighty hunter “before the Lord” (Genesis 10:9), a phrase that carries defiance more than admiration. Under his rule, humanity attempted the first global rebellion by constructing a tower to the heavens—not to honour God, but to challenge Him.
“And they said, Go to, let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven… lest we be scattered.”
— Genesis 11:4
This was more than architecture. It was the first mystery religion, rooted in astrology, self-deification, and cosmic rebellion.
God scattered them, confounded their languages, and Babel became Babylon—not just a city, but a spiritual archetype for every false system to follow.
